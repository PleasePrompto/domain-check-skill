# What Makes a Great Domain Name in 2025/2026

A practical guide distilled from 20+ current sources on naming psychology, branding,
and registrar best practices. Use this as a creative starting point before generating
candidates with `domain_check.py suggest`.

## TL;DR — The 7 Rules That Matter Most

1. **Pass the type-once test.** If a stranger hears it once and types it without asking "how do you spell that?", it lives. If not, it dies.
2. **Aim for 5-10 characters.** The top-100 sites average 6.2; YC's top 100 average 5.8. Every character past seven costs you traffic.
3. **Open with a plosive (P, T, K, B, D, G).** Plosives correlate with brand profitability and were one of the two most common features of the Marketing & Media Decisions Top 200 from 1971-1985. Think Pinterest, Tesla, Google, Tumblr, TikTok.
4. **End on a vowel for stickiness.** Tesla, Hinge, Stripe, Nvidia, Notion, Figma, Tesla, Adobe, Roku, Tableau. Open syllables linger; closed consonants die fast.
5. **Buy the .com if you ever want to scale past developers.** GrowthBadger and most trust studies still rank .com #1. Use .ai/.io/.dev only when they sharpen the signal to your audience.
6. **Never use hyphens, digit-letter swaps, or disemvoweled cleverness.** "4u", "biz-name", and "Flickr-style" missing letters all leak traffic to competitors who own the obvious spelling.
7. **Say it out loud, in three languages, to your grandma.** If she asks you to repeat it, if it sounds dirty in Spanish, or if it rhymes with a competitor — kill it.

## 1. The Type-Once Test

The single most important filter. A domain name only works if a person who heard it spoken at a podcast, a meetup, or a sales call can type the URL correctly without a second prompt.

Brands that pass:

- **Stripe** — one short English word, zero spelling ambiguity.
- **Notion** — one phonetic word, ends in a vowel sound.
- **Figma** — invented but spells exactly how it sounds.
- **Zoom** — onomatopoeic, four letters, impossible to misspell.
- **Hinge** — single concrete word, no homophones that matter.

Brands that almost fail:

- **Xero** — accounting software. Constant "is that with a Z or an X?" friction.
- **Squarespace** — long, but at least the two halves are common English.

Testing protocol: dictate the candidate to three people over the phone and watch them type it. Anything below 100% accuracy goes back into the pile. Spaceship's own analysis of one-word domains like twitch.tv and hinge.co frames this as **cognitive fluency** — the brain rewards what it processes effortlessly.

## 2. Length Sweet Spots

Hard data from multiple registrars and academic sources:

- **Top 100 websites** average **6.2 characters** before the dot.
- **Fortune 500** average **6.5 characters**.
- **Y Combinator top 100** average **5.8 characters**.
- **Average .com registration**: 13.5 characters — a graveyard of forgettable names.
- **University of Pennsylvania study**: a 2% traffic reduction for every character past the seventh.
- **Nielsen Norman Group**: users prefer 3-5 letter words to 8-9 letter words for recall.
- **Miller's Law (1956)**: working memory holds 7 ± 2 chunks. Domain names that exceed this leak from short-term memory before the user reaches the URL bar.

**Rule of thumb:** 4-8 characters is gold, 9-12 is workable if memorable, 13+ is only acceptable if every word is a dictionary common word (e.g., `squarespace.com`, `mailchimp.com`).

## 3. Phonetics & Sound

### Plosives at the start

Plosive consonants — /p/, /t/, /k/, /b/, /d/, /g/ — explode air through the mouth and force attention. Klink (2001) and follow-up research show plosives correlate with perceptions of strength, precision, and modernity, and with measurable lifts in **brand recall and profitability**.

- /K/ openers: Kodak, Kleenex, Coca-Cola, Canva, Klarna.
- /P/ openers: Pepsi, Pinterest, PayPal, Patagonia, Plaid.
- /T/ openers: Tesla, TikTok, Twitter, Tumblr, Twilio.
- /B/, /D/, /G/: Bose, Dove, Google, Discord, Datadog.

A 2024 sound-symbolism study cited by The Identity Bureau found that plosive-led names showed **43% higher recognition** among 18-34 year olds compared to fricative- or sibilant-led equivalents.

### Vowel endings make names sticky

Open syllables (vowel-final) ring on in working memory. Closed syllables (consonant-final) get clipped.

- Vowel-final winners: Tesla, Honda, Toyota, Nvidia, Notion, Figma, Stripe (silent-e closed but phonetically vowel-ish), Adobe, Roku, Datadog... wait — Datadog is consonant-final but its compound rhythm carries it.
- Tesla > Tesl. Notion > Notn. Honda > Hond. The vowel ending feels finished.

### Alliteration and rhyme

Repetition is the cheapest mnemonic in linguistics.

- Alliteration: Coca-Cola, PayPal, Krispy Kreme, Range Rover, Best Buy, TikTok, Dunkin' Donuts.
- Rhyme: Reese's Pieces, Slim Jim, Hubba Bubba, Stub Hub.

Argo's 2010 *Journal of Marketing* study showed brands evaluated **most favorably** when phonetic structure contains repetition AND the name is spoken aloud.

## 4. Naming Patterns That Work

### Real-Word Brands

A common dictionary word reframed for a new category. Cheapest mnemonic, hardest to acquire.

- Apple, Amazon, Shell, Visa, Oracle, Hinge, Bumble, Slack, Square, Stripe.

Pros: instant recognition, easy spelling, free advertising every time the word appears in everyday life.
Cons: rarely available as .com under $50k. Trademark fights are bloody — you're claiming a generic word in one class while everyone else uses it elsewhere.

### Compound Brands

Two real words mashed together. The workhorse of modern naming.

- Facebook, YouTube, Snowflake, Salesforce, Mailchimp, GraphBase, BioThread, GitHub, Substack, Airtable.

Pros: descriptive, semi-available, easy to trademark when the combination is novel.
Cons: longer (8-12 chars common), can feel literal/uninspired if the two halves are obvious (`onlinepetfood.com`).

### Made-Up / Phonetic

Pure invention with no dictionary meaning. Maximum trademark protection, maximum brand-equity ceiling.

- Kodak (deliberately invented by George Eastman to be language-neutral), Xerox, Häagen-Dazs, Spotify, Twilio, Zillow, Roblox, Verizon.

Pros: clean trademark, .com almost always available, no false-friend baggage.
Cons: meaningless on day one — you have to spend on awareness. Risk of mispronunciation (Twilio = TWIL-ee-oh, not TWY-lee-oh).

### Portmanteau

Two-word blend where source words are recoverable. Daniel Eckler's rule: "a good portmanteau sounds like it was a word already."

- Microsoft (microcomputer + software), Pinterest (pin + interest), Groupon (group + coupon), Instagram (instant + telegram), Netflix (internet + flicks), Wayfair (way + fair), Wikipedia (wiki + encyclopedia), Verizon (veritas + horizon).

Pros: meaning + originality, easy trademark.
Cons: can feel contrived if the seam shows.

### Greek/Latin Roots — the 2026 trend ("Hybrid Neoclassicism")

The dominant naming aesthetic in 2025-2026 venture-backed startups. Pure Latin (`Veritas`, `Solaris`) is out — too monastic. The trend is a **Greek/Latin morpheme glued to a modern tech noun**.

- **Lumen** (Latin "light") — telecom, fiber optics.
- **Nexus** (Latin "connection") — networking platforms.
- **GraphBase, BioThread, MetaForge, NeoCloud, AxonOps, OmniLayer, HelioGrid, CryoTrace.**
- The morpheme inventory: *bio-, neo-, omni-, helio-, axo-, cryo-, lumi-, nexa-, meta-, proto-, kine-, photo-, lex-*.

Why it's working in 2026: Per the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute (2025), brands with traceable etymology are **22% more likely to surface in AI-generated "top recommendations"**. A Gartner consumer report found **68% of Gen Z entrepreneurs prefer names with traceable origins**. Latin/Greek roots are LLM-friendly — the model can disambiguate them from common-noun queries.

### Action Verbs

Imperative verbs that feel kinetic. A mid-2010s pattern still alive.

- Stripe, Slack, Lyft, Lift, Drift, Sprint, Snap, Shop, Fetch, Click, Stash, Spark, Pluck.

## 5. TLD Strategy 2026

| Use case | Primary | Acceptable | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mass-consumer brand, B2C | **.com** | .co | .biz, .info, .xyz |
| AI startup (developer/B2B) | **.ai** | .com (with `get/use` prefix) | .io for AI-pure plays |
| Developer tool / CLI | **.dev** | **.io**, .com | .net |
| Mobile app | **.app** | .com | .mobi |
| German market | **.de** | .com | .com.de |
| UK market | **.co.uk** or **.uk** | .com | unrelated ccTLDs |
| French / Swiss / Austrian | **.fr / .ch / .at** | .com | random gTLD |
| Personal portfolio / creator | .me, .blog, .design | .com | .info |
| Domain investor / auction | **.com** only | — | everything else |

**The .com test.** GrowthBadger's trust survey still has .com as the most trusted extension by a wide margin. If your audience includes anyone over 35 or any non-technical buyer, the .com is non-negotiable for your primary URL.

**The .ai surge.** As of 2025 H1, **28% of Y Combinator and Techstars startups** use .ai. In 2024 YC's Winter batch, 132 of 248 companies were on .com, but 58 were on .ai — a higher density than any prior year. .ai is now a **category signal**, not a fallback.

**The .io question.** .io is the British Indian Ocean Territory ccTLD. It carried the developer-startup crown 2014-2022. In 2026 it's losing prestige to .ai (for AI plays) and .dev (for tooling). There are also persistent geopolitical concerns about its long-term stability.

**ccTLD trust.** A .de domain on a German business is a stronger trust signal than a .com — locals expect it. Same for .fr in France, .co.uk in Britain, .ch in Switzerland.

**The "get/use" workaround.** 54% of YC .com-using companies in 2024 had a non-exact-match URL like `getbrand.com` or `usebrand.com`. Acceptable as a launch hack — but rebrand to the exact match before Series B.

## 6. The AI-Era Twist

Three new pressures the 2010s naming playbooks didn't anticipate:

1. **LLM citability.** When ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini answer "what's the best X?", they cite by *brand name string*, not by URL. Names that are unique tokens (Notion, Figma, Linear, Cursor) are easier for an LLM to retrieve than common-word names ("Apple" returns the fruit and the company; "Stream" returns thousands of products). Made-up and Greek/Latin-rooted names have a measurable AI-discoverability edge.

2. **Voice agents.** Siri, Alexa, and now agentic AI assistants book and buy on your behalf. They parse your spoken request into a domain. Names with homophones (Site/Sight, Lyft/Lift) lose to the literal spelling. The disemvoweling era (Flickr, Tumblr, Grindr) is decisively over — voice agents transcribe vowels, not stylized brand spellings.

3. **AI search snippets.** Brand names that include their category (`graphbase.dev`, `axonops.com`) get cited more often when the LLM is summarizing a category, because the name itself reinforces the topic. This is a soft return of "keyword in domain" — but as a *brand* signal, not an SEO one.

## 7. The Don't List

- **No hyphens.** They destroy CTR, signal "spam-adjacent," and lose word-of-mouth ("dash" or "minus"?) every time.
- **No digit substitutions.** `4u`, `2go`, `numbr`, `biz4you` — voice agents and Google both deprecate these.
- **No disemvoweled cleverness in 2026.** Flickr worked in 2004 because the .com they wanted was taken. It does not work today; pick a longer or invented name.
- **No confusables.** Lowercase L vs uppercase I (Illy vs lily), zero vs O, rn vs m. Every confusable spawns a typo-squat.
- **Trademark traps.** USPTO TESS, EUIPO eSearch, and a quick Google check are mandatory before you spend on a domain. Even if the .com is for sale, a class-overlap trademark holder can sink you.
- **Typo-squat distance ≥ 2.** Your name should be at least Levenshtein-distance 2 from any well-known existing brand. `Stripee.com`, `Notyon.com`, `Figmma.com` invite expensive lawsuits.
- **No false friends.** Mitsubishi Pajero became Montero in Spanish markets because *pajero* is vulgar slang. Reebok's Incubus was a women's running shoe named after a demon that rapes sleeping women. Test in Spanish, German, French, Mandarin, and Japanese minimum before going global.
- **No tortured spellings.** Kwik, Lyte, Foto, Krazy. Voice search and dictation murder these.

## 8. How the Pros Brainstorm

Real exercises used inside Lexicon, Igor, Catchword, and Eat My Words:

- **Mood board.** Lay out 30-50 images that capture the brand's emotional target — colors, textures, scenes, people. Generate 100 words off the visuals before any letter touches paper.
- **Sound clusters.** Pick a target phoneme set (e.g., plosive opener + vowel ending + 2 syllables). Force yourself to invent 50 candidates within those rails.
- **Semantic web.** Start from the core noun ("speed"). Branch into synonyms (swift, fleet, nimble), antonyms (slow → ironic plays), and adjacent metaphors (lightning, cheetah, arrow, comet). Each branch is a name pool.
- **Free association.** "If this brand were an animal / weapon / drink / planet / celebrity, which one?" Five rounds of this against five framings produces ~125 raw seeds.
- **The grandma test.** Read your top 10 to a non-technical relative. The ones they remember an hour later survive.
- **The 1AM bar test.** Can a slightly drunk friend type the URL into their phone in a noisy bar without asking you to repeat it? If not, kill it.
- **Linguistic screening.** Run the shortlist through a native speaker of Spanish, French, German, Mandarin, Japanese, and Arabic. Naming agency Nomen does this with an in-house panel of 100+ languages — small startups can do it on Upwork in an afternoon.

## 9. Multilingual Pitfalls

Real failures and what they teach:

- **Mitsubishi Pajero** — Spanish slang for "wanker." Rebranded as Montero. Lesson: any word that exists in another major language must be looked up in slang dictionaries, not just standard ones.
- **Reebok Incubus** — a demon associated with sleep assault. Pulled within weeks. Lesson: check English mythology and folklore, not just foreign translations.
- **Honda Fitta** — Swedish/Norwegian vulgarity for female anatomy. Renamed Jazz/Fit. Lesson: Scandinavian languages bite hard on short vowel-rich names.
- **Nokia Lumia** — Spanish slang for "prostitute" in some regions. Lesson: regional Spanish varies wildly; check Latin American AND European Spanish.
- **Pajero, Nova, Fitta, Lumia, Incubus** all share a pattern: short, vowel-rich names sound great in English and collide with other languages exactly because vowel-rich short words are the densest part of any phonetic space.

The Chevy Nova "no va" story is mostly apocryphal — Spanish speakers don't actually parse "Nova" as "no va" — but it's still cited because the principle is real: any short brand can collide with the morphology of another language.

## 10. Brandable vs Keyword Domains

The 2026 SEO reality: **Google's Exact-Match Domain Update (2012)** plus the Helpful Content Updates (2022-2024) effectively neutered exact-match domains as a ranking factor. Keywords in the domain are now a **small relevance signal** — not a boost.

What this means in practice:

- **Brandable wins long-term.** Stripe, Notion, Figma, Linear ranked their categories by being *brands*, not by being `paymentprocessing.com`.
- **EMDs still convert better in cold paid traffic.** If a user searches "best running shoes" and sees `bestrunningshoes.com` in the SERP, CTR rises ~5-10%. Useful for short-term campaigns or affiliate sites.
- **Partial-match is the modern hybrid.** `figmadesign.com`, `linearapp.com`, `notion.so` — keyword adjacent without sacrificing brand.
- **Branded clicks compound.** Once a brand has direct-search demand (people typing the name), Google treats brand strength as a major ranking signal. EMDs without brand strength stagnate.

Decision rule: if you plan to build a venture-backed company → brandable. If you plan to flip an affiliate site in 12 months → EMD.

| Relentless.com | Amazon.com | 1995 | Bezos wanted A-first for early-internet alphabetical listings; "Amazon" connoted scale. |

## 12. A 30-Minute Naming Workout

Do this alone, with paper, before opening any AI generator.

- **Minutes 0-3.** Write the *one sentence* describing what your product does. Underline the noun and the verb.
- **Minutes 3-8.** Write 30 synonyms for that noun and 30 for that verb. Use a thesaurus — fast, no judgment.
- **Minutes 8-13.** Pick 5 favorites from each list. For each, write its Latin and Greek roots (etymonline.com is your friend).
- **Minutes 13-18.** Free-associate: if your product were an animal, weapon, drink, planet, mythological figure, color — list one per category. 6 metaphors.
- **Minutes 18-23.** Combine. Smash a Greek/Latin root onto a tech noun. Smash a metaphor onto a verb. Generate 40 candidates without filtering.
- **Minutes 23-26.** Score each candidate against: type-once test (1 point), 4-8 chars (1), plosive opener (1), vowel ending (1), .com or strong alt available (1). Anything below 4/5 dies.
- **Minutes 26-29.** Read survivors aloud. Dictate to a friend over text or call. Watch the typing.
- **Minute 30.** Top three go to USPTO TESS, EUIPO, Google, and a domain checker. The first one that survives all four is your starting candidate.

Repeat the workout three times across three days. The brain produces fundamentally different word pools when rested.
